## 1. INTERVIEW OVERVIEW

### Purpose
Capture lived experiences, perceptions, and narratives of classical guitar learning and concierto performance development across the 4-year diploma program.

### Interview Types
- **Student Interviews**: Individual experiences (semi-structured, 45-60 minutes)
- **Teacher Interviews**: Pedagogical observations (semi-structured, 30-45 minutes)
- **Focus Group Interviews** (Optional): Group discussion of shared experiences (60-90 minutes)

### Timing
- **Semester-based**: Conduct at end of each semester (S1, S3, S5, S7)
- **Longitudinal**: Same students across all years for comparison
- **Total per student**: 4 interviews over 4 years

### Sample Size
- **Students**: 8-12 interviews (varied trajectory types)
- **Teachers**: 2-3 interviews
- **Focus Groups**: 1-2 groups of 5-6 students each

### Ethical Considerations
✓ IRB approval obtained
✓ Informed consent signed
✓ Confidentiality assured
✓ Right to withdraw at any time
✓ Audio recording permission obtained
✓ Data stored securely

## 2. STUDENT INTERVIEW GUIDE

### Interview Structure

**Opening (5 minutes)**
- Welcome and rapport building
- Confirm audio recording consent
- Explain confidentiality
- Overview of interview topics

**Main Interview (40-50 minutes)**
- Learning experience and progression
- Challenges and breakthroughs
- Practice strategies
- Concierto-specific development
- Performance confidence
- Teacher influence
- Peer relationships
- Future goals

**Closing (5 minutes)**
- Ask if anything else to share
- Thank you and next steps
- Confirm contact information

### Section-by-Section Interview Questions

---

### SECTION A: BACKGROUND & MOTIVATION

**Purpose**: Understand student background and initial motivations

**A1. Musical Background**
- "Tell me about your musical background before entering this program."
- "How many years had you played guitar before starting the diploma?"
- "Did you have any formal training before? If so, what style?"
- "Why did you choose classical guitar specifically?"

**A2. Program Selection**
- "What motivated you to pursue a diploma in classical guitar?"
- "What were your initial goals when you started the program?"
- "How have those goals changed over time?"
- "What does 'success' mean to you in classical guitar?"

**A3. Family & Support**
- "How did your family react to your choice of studying classical guitar?"
- "Is there financial support for your studies?"
- "Do you have people who support your musical journey?"

---

### SECTION B: LEARNING EXPERIENCE

**Purpose**: Understand the overall learning journey

**B1. General Learning Experience**
- "How would you describe your learning experience over the past [semester/year]?"
- "What aspects of learning have been most enjoyable?"
- "What has been most challenging?"
- "How has your approach to learning changed since you started?"

**B2. Confidence Development**
- "How confident do you feel about your abilities right now?"
- "Can you describe a moment when you felt a significant confidence boost?"
- "Have there been periods of self-doubt? How did you handle them?"
- "How do you compare your current confidence to when you started?"

**B3. Motivation & Engagement**
- "What keeps you motivated to continue studying guitar?"
- "Are there times when motivation dips? How do you handle that?"
- "What aspects of the program most excite you?"
- "If you could change one thing about the program, what would it be?"

---

### SECTION C: TECHNICAL SKILL DEVELOPMENT

**Purpose**: Understand technical progression and skill acquisition

**C1. Technical Progress**
- "What technical skills do you feel you've developed most strongly?"
- "What technical areas still challenge you?"
- "Can you describe a technical skill that was difficult and how you mastered it?"
- "How do you approach learning difficult technical passages?"

**C2. Practice Strategies**
- "Walk me through your typical practice session."
- "How long do you practice on an average day?"
- "How has your practice approach changed over the semesters?"
- "What practice strategies have been most effective for you?"
- "Do you use any specific techniques for memorization?"

**C3. Finger Work & Technique**
- "How comfortable are you with higher position work?"
- "What about rapid string crossing? How has that developed?"
- "Describe your experience learning complex fingering patterns."
- "Have you experienced any physical challenges (hand pain, tension)?"

**C4. Performance Technique**
- "How do you handle performance mistakes?"
- "What's the difference between how you play in practice vs. performance?"
- "How do you manage nerves before performing?"
- "What techniques help you maintain consistency?"

---

### SECTION D: ARTISTIC DEVELOPMENT

**Purpose**: Understand musical interpretation and artistic growth

**D1. Musicality & Interpretation**
- "How has your understanding of musical phrasing changed?"
- "Can you describe your interpretation approach to a concierto?"
- "How do you decide on tempo, dynamics, and phrasing?"
- "Has your interpretation of pieces changed over time? How?"

**D2. Emotional Expression**
- "How do you connect emotionally with the music you play?"
- "Can you describe a moment when you felt fully connected to a piece?"
- "How do you convey emotion through guitar performance?"
- "What role does emotion play in your practice?"

**D3. Personal Musical Voice**
- "Do you feel you've developed a personal musical voice?"
- "How would you describe your unique interpretation style?"
- "How did your style develop? Who influenced it?"
- "How does your interpretation differ from your teacher's or other students'?"

**D4. Composer Understanding**
- "How do you research composers and their works?"
- "How has understanding historical context changed your interpretation?"
- "Which composers resonate most with you? Why?"
- "How do you balance historical accuracy with personal expression?"

### SECTION E: CONCIERTO-SPECIFIC EXPERIENCE

**Purpose**: Capture concierto-specific learning and challenges

**E1. Concierto Selection**

- "How difficult was each concierto compared to previous repertoire?"

- "How did you prepare mentally for starting a new concierto?"

**E2. Concierto Learning Process**

- "What are the unique challenges of conciertos vs. other repertoire?"
- "How long did it take to learn each concierto?"
- "What parts of the concierto were most challenging?"

**E3. Concierto Performance**
- "Describe your experience performing conciertos in concerts."
- "How different is performing a concierto from performing a short piece?"
- "What preparation do you do before a concierto performance?"
- "Can you describe the feeling of performing a complete concierto?"

**E4. Concierto Progression**
- "How has your approach to conciertos changed over the years?"
- "How do current conciertos compare to ones you learned earlier?"
- "What has become easier about concierto performance?"
- "What remains challenging?"

## 4. INTERVIEW ADMINISTRATION

### Pre-Interview Preparation

**For Researcher:**
- [ ] Review student profile/background
- [ ] Prepare interview protocol
- [ ] Test recording device
- [ ] Arrange quiet location
- [ ] Set up comfortable seating
- [ ] Have consent forms ready
- [ ] Prepare notes paper

**Environment:**
- Quiet, private location
- Comfortable temperature
- Good acoustics for recording
- Limited distractions
- Professional but relaxed atmosphere

### Interview Procedures

**Before Recording:**
1. Welcome and introduce yourself
2. Explain study purpose
3. Review consent form
4. Confirm audio recording permission
5. Discuss confidentiality
6. Answer questions

**During Recording:**
1. Start recording and note time
2. Ask opening question
3. Listen actively
4. Use probes: "Tell me more," "Can you give an example?"
5. Note non-verbal cues
6. Allow silence for reflection
7. Follow interesting tangents
8. Watch for time

**After Recording:**
1. Stop recording, note end time
2. Ask if anything else to share
3. Thank participant
4. Discuss next steps
5. Collect contact info for follow-up

### Recording & Notes

**Audio Recording:**
- Use high-quality recorder
- Test audio levels
- Minimize background noise
- Save with naming convention: Student_ID_Semester_Date.mp3
- Create backup copy

**Field Notes:**
- Note participant reactions
- Record significant pauses or emotions
- Note non-verbal communication
- Record environmental factors
- Note time stamps for important quotes

## 5. QUESTION LIBRARY

### Probing Techniques

**Open-Ended Probes:**
- "Tell me more about that..."
- "Can you give me an example?"
- "What happened next?"
- "How did that feel?"
- "What do you mean by...?"

**Clarifying Probes:**
- "When you say X, do you mean Y or Z?"
- "Is that different from...?"
- "So you're saying...?"
- "Help me understand..."

**Deepening Probes:**
- "That's interesting, why do you think that is?"
- "How does that connect to...?"
- "What would have happened if...?"
- "How often does that happen?"

**Comparison Probes:**
- "How is that different from...?"
- "Compare that to your experience with...?"
- "Has that always been true, or has it changed?"
- "How does that compare to [other semester]?"

## 6. CODING & ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK

### Major Themes for Analysis

**Theme 1: Learning Progression**
- Code: PROG
- Sub-codes: PROG-TECH (technical), PROG-ART (artistic), PROG-PERF (performance)

**Theme 2: Challenges**
- Code: CHAL
- Sub-codes: CHAL-TECH, CHAL-PSYCH, CHAL-PRACTICE, CHAL-SOCIAL

**Theme 3: Breakthroughs**
- Code: BT
- Sub-codes: BT-MOMENT, BT-GROWTH, BT-MOTIVATION

**Theme 4: Teaching Influence**
- Code: TEACH
- Sub-codes: TEACH-METHOD, TEACH-FEEDBACK, TEACH-RELATIONSHIP

**Theme 5: Performance Experience**
- Code: PERF
- Sub-codes: PERF-ANXIETY, PERF-SUCCESS, PERF-GROWTH

**Theme 6: Peer/Community**
- Code: COMM
- Sub-codes: COMM-PEER, COMM-ENSEMBLE, COMM-SUPPORT

**Theme 7: Future Orientation**
- Code: FUT
- Sub-codes: FUT-CAREER, FUT-LEARNING, FUT-GOALS

### Coding Process

1. **First Pass**: Read entire transcript
2. **Mark Significant Passages**: Highlight relevant quotes
3. **Initial Codes**: Assign preliminary codes
4. **Check Consistency**: Ensure codes used consistently
5. **Refine Codes**: Create new codes if needed
6. **Organize**: Group by theme
7. **Analysis**: Look for patterns across participants

### Example Coded Passage

```
Interview Excerpt:
"By semester 4, I realized I could actually play the piece musically,
not just technically. That was a turning point for me."

Codes: BT (Breakthrough), PROG-ART (Artistic Progression), 
BT-MOMENT (Breakthrough Moment), TEACH-EFFECT (Teaching Effect)

Meaning: Student experienced significant breakthrough in moving from 
technical to artistic focus, critical transition at semester 4.
```

## 7. SAMPLE INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT

### Interview: Student ID STU-005 (Type B - Breakthrough Performer)
**Date**: May 20, 2026 | **Semester**: 4 | **Duration**: 52 minutes

---

**INTERVIEWER (I):** Good morning! Thank you so much for taking time to meet with me. I appreciate your willingness to participate in this study. Before we start, let me just confirm—is it okay if I record this conversation?

**STUDENT (S):** Yes, that's fine.

**I:** Great. So as I mentioned, I'm studying how classical guitar students develop over the four-year program, particularly focusing on concierto performance. I'm interested in understanding your experience—both the challenges and the exciting moments. There are no right or wrong answers; I just want to hear your perspective.

**S:** Okay, I understand.

**I:** Let me start with some background. Tell me about your musical journey before you came to the university.

**S:** Well, I started guitar when I was about 10 years old. My parents signed me up for lessons at a local music school. I played electric guitar for a couple years, actually, before switching to classical. [BACKGROUND]

**I:** What made you switch?

**S:** My teacher suggested it. She said my fingers were suited for classical, and honestly, I think she was right. The classical technique just felt more... natural? More challenging in a good way.

**I:** And how long had you been playing classical before the diploma program?

**S:** Maybe seven or eight years by the time I started here. So I had a pretty solid foundation, but I wasn't at an advanced level yet. [BACKGROUND-PRIOR]

**I:** Got it. So you came in with good foundational skills. What made you decide to pursue a diploma in classical guitar?

**S:** I loved playing, but I wasn't sure if I wanted to pursue it professionally. My parents were a bit concerned about a music career. But I wanted to see if I could really develop at a higher level. The diploma seemed like a good option—more flexible than a full degree, but serious enough to really push myself. [MOTIVATION-GOALS]

**I:** That's helpful. Now let me ask you about your learning experience over the past four semesters. How would you describe the journey so far?

**S:** [Long pause] It's been... honestly, it's been more difficult than I expected, but also more rewarding. The first two semesters were really about establishing basics—good posture, solid technique, learning the fundamentals of more complex pieces. [PROG-EARLY]

**I:** And how did that go?

**S:** Pretty smoothly, actually. I felt like I was learning a lot, but I wasn't really... connecting with the music yet, if that makes sense? I was focused so much on not making mistakes that I wasn't really listening to what I was playing. [EARLY-LIMITATION]

**I:** That's insightful. When did that change for you?

**S:** [Pause] In semester 3, actually. We started learning the Castelnuovo-Tedesco Concerto. [CONCIERTO-SPECIFIC] At first, I was overwhelmed. The piece is SO much longer than anything I'd worked on before. The technical demands are higher. I remember thinking, "I can't do this." [CHAL-INITIAL]

**I:** That sounds discouraging. How did you work through that?

**S:** [Laughs] Well, my teacher—she's great—she helped me see that I didn't need to learn the whole piece at once. We broke it into sections. And she did something really important. [TEACH-METHOD] She had me listen to professional recordings, not to copy them, but to understand what the piece was trying to say. She asked me, "What emotion does this section convey to you?" 

I think that's when things shifted for me. [BT-MOMENT] I started practicing with intention. I wasn't just playing notes; I was trying to express something. And suddenly, the technical stuff that had seemed impossible started becoming possible. [BT-GROWTH]

**I:** That's fascinating. So the shift was from technical focus to musical/emotional focus?

**S:** Yes, exactly. And once I made that shift, my practice became so much more efficient. I would practice fewer hours, but more intentionally. [PRACTICE-CHANGE] By semester 4, I felt like I was really playing the piece, not just performing it.

**I:** Can you describe what that difference feels like to you?

**S:** The difference between performing and playing? [Thinks] When you're just performing, you're thinking about technical execution—"Am I hitting the right notes? Are my fingers in position?" You're in your head. [PERF-TECH] But when you're really playing, you're connected to the emotion. Your technique becomes automatic, and you can focus on expression. [PERF-MUSIC] It's like... the piece flows through you rather than you pushing through it.

**I:** Beautiful way to describe it. Did that change how you felt about performance?

**S:** Definitely. [PERF-ANXIETY-DECREASE] I was really nervous before my first concierto performance in semester 3. But in semester 4, I still had nerves, but they were different. They were excitement nerves, not fear nerves. [CONFIDENCE-GROWTH] I felt prepared not just technically, but musically. I knew what I was trying to communicate.

**I:** Have you experienced similar breakthroughs with other pieces?

**S:** [Nods] With semester 5, when I started working on the Vivaldi, I applied the same approach right from the beginning. Instead of learning the technique first and then adding emotion, I was thinking about emotion and style right from the start. [LEARNING-INTEGRATION] It's made the whole learning process faster and more enjoyable.

**I:** That's great learning. What about challenges? Are there still things that feel difficult?

**S:** Of course! [Laughs] There are still technical passages that challenge me. The fast string crossings in one section of the Castelnuovo-Tedesco still require really focused practice. [CHAL-TECH-ONGOING] But it's different now. I don't feel defeated by the challenge. I feel like I have tools to work through it.

**I:** What tools?

**S:** Slow practice, breaking passages into smaller segments, thinking about the musical purpose of the passage. [PRACTICE-STRATEGY] Also, my teacher helps me see technical challenges not as limitations but as opportunities for development.

**I:** How has your teacher specifically supported your development?

**S:** In so many ways. [TEACH-RELATIONSHIP] The most important thing is that she believes in my potential. Even in semester 2 when I was frustrated, she kept saying, "This will click for you." And it did. [TEACH-ENCOURAGEMENT] She's also really skilled at giving feedback. She doesn't just tell me what's wrong; she shows me what's possible. She'll play a passage, then say, "Now you try, and listen for these specific things..." [TEACH-FEEDBACK-SPECIFIC]

**I:** Has your approach to practice changed over the semesters?

**S:** Significantly. [PRACTICE-EVOLUTION] Early on, I thought more hours meant better progress. I'd practice 3-4 hours and still feel unproductive. Now I practice 2-2.5 hours, but much more focused. I write down specific goals for the practice session. [PRACTICE-GOAL-ORIENTED] I record myself regularly to hear what's actually happening vs. what I think is happening. [PRACTICE-SELF-MONITORING] I also pay attention to when I'm tired or frustrated and take breaks. [PRACTICE-MINDFUL]

**I:** Do you practice the same way for all pieces, or do you adjust your approach?

**S:** I adjust based on what the piece needs. [PRACTICE-ADAPTIVE] For the conciertos, which are long and complex, I focus a lot on musical phrasing and structure. For shorter technical pieces, I might focus more on finger work and speed. [PRACTICE-DIFFERENTIATED]

**I:** I notice you mentioned recording yourself. Why is that important?

**S:** [Thoughtful] Because what you think you're playing and what you're actually playing are often different. [SELF-AWARENESS] When I listen to a recording, I hear things I missed—tempo inconsistencies, dynamic control issues, places where I rush or slow down. [FEEDBACK-OBJECTIVE] It's humbling sometimes, but it's so valuable.

**I:** How do you handle that gap between what you think you're doing and what you're actually doing?

**S:** At first, it was discouraging. [EARLY-SELF-DOUBT] But my teacher reframed it. She said, "The fact that you can hear the difference means you're developing your musical ear. That's a skill." [REFRAMING] Now I see recordings as a teaching tool, not a judgment. [GROWTH-MINDSET]

**I:** That's mature musicianship. Let me ask about performance. You mentioned nerves earlier. How do you manage them?

**S:** I have a pre-performance ritual. [COPING-RITUAL] About 15 minutes before I perform, I go somewhere quiet. I do some deep breathing. I visualize playing a passage I feel confident about. [VISUALIZATION] Then I remind myself why I love this piece. [EMOTIONAL-CONNECTION] I tell myself, "I've prepared this. I know this music. Now just share it."

**I:** Does that help?

**S:** Most of the time, yes. [ANXIETY-MANAGEMENT] I still get nervous—I think any musician does. But the ritual helps me channel that nervous energy into excitement. [ENERGY-CHANNELING] And if I do make a mistake in performance, I've learned not to let it derail me. [RECOVERY-RESILIENCE] I just keep going and focus on the next phrase.

**I:** Have you had a performance that didn't go as planned?

**S:** Yes, actually. In semester 3, my first concierto performance in a formal setting—I blanked on a section. [CHALLENGE-INCIDENT] My mind just went blank for maybe three seconds, which felt like an eternity. [IMPACT] But I kept going, and honestly, I think most people didn't notice. [PERSPECTIVE]

**I:** What did you learn from that?

**S:** That perfection isn't the goal. [LESSON-REFRAME] Connection and communication are the goals. The audience would rather hear a human performance with a small mistake than a robotic perfect performance. [VALUE-SHIFT] And I learned that I'm more resilient than I thought. [CONFIDENCE-GROWTH-2]

**I:** That's wise. Let me ask about your peers. Do you interact much with other classical guitar students?

**S:** A bit, yes. [PEER-COMMUNITY] There are maybe four other guitar students in our cohort, and we support each other. [PEER-SUPPORT] Sometimes there's a little bit of friendly competition, which keeps me motivated. [PEER-MOTIVATION] And it's nice to know I'm not alone in struggling with pieces or dealing with anxiety. [PEER-NORMALIZATION]

**I:** Do you practice together or perform together?

**S:** Not really in formal ensembles, but we'll sometimes get together and play for each other, give feedback. [PEER-LEARNING] That's been valuable too, hearing how others interpret pieces.

**I:** As you look ahead, what are your plans after the diploma?

**S:** I'm actually thinking about pursuing a bachelor's degree now. [FUT-EDUCATION] I didn't think I wanted that at first, but this experience has changed my thinking. [MOTIVATION-GROWTH] I'd like to eventually teach, and also continue performing. Maybe not as a professional soloist—I think I'd burn out—but regular chamber groups, maybe community orchestras. [FUT-GOALS-REVISED] And I want to keep developing as a musician.

**I:** Is there anything else you'd like to share?

**S:** Just that this program has been transformative. [REFLECTION] I came in thinking I knew a lot about playing guitar, but I didn't really understand musicianship. This experience, especially the shift in semester 3-4, has taught me that musicianship is just as important, maybe more important, than technique. [KEY-LEARNING]

**I:** Thank you so much. This is incredibly valuable. Is it okay if I contact you for a follow-up conversation in a few months?

**S:** Of course, I'd be happy to.

## 8. FOLLOW-UP PROTOCOLS

### Immediate Follow-Up (Within 1 week)
- Transcribe audio recording
- Add field notes to transcript
- Check for missing information
- Send thank-you note to participant

### Data Verification (Within 2 weeks)
- Contact participant if clarification needed
- Verify quotes in context
- Confirm interpretations
- Member checking conversation

### Analysis Phase (Within 4 weeks)
- Code interview
- Identify themes
- Note patterns
- Compare with other interviews

### Longitudinal Tracking (For semester interviews)
- Schedule next interview
- Reference previous interviews
- Track changes over time
- Build narrative arc

### Final Follow-Up (After program completion)
- Share key findings with participant
- Thank them for participation
- Discuss any confidentiality concerns
- Offer to share final report

## APPENDIX: QUICK REFERENCE CARDS

### Interview Tips Checklist
- [ ] Create comfortable environment
- [ ] Use high-quality recording device
- [ ] Take field notes on non-verbal cues
- [ ] Ask open-ended questions
- [ ] Listen more than you talk
- [ ] Use probing questions appropriately
- [ ] Allow silence for reflection
- [ ] Note significant quotes
- [ ] Watch time without rushing
- [ ] End with "Is there anything else?"

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid
❌ Asking leading questions
❌ Interrupting participant
❌ Dominating the conversation
❌ Showing judgment or bias
❌ Poor audio quality
❌ Rushing through interview
❌ Failing to get consent
❌ Sharing confidential information

### Active Listening Techniques
✓ Maintain eye contact
✓ Use non-verbal encouragement (nods, "mm-hmm")
✓ Paraphrase to confirm understanding
✓ Note body language
✓ Let participant finish thoughts
✓ Ask clarifying questions
✓ Show genuine interest
✓ Create space for reflection
